## 🏠 Ring’s Jamie Siminoff is still trying to calm privacy fears, but his answers may not help

Let’s be real: having a smart doorbell feels like having a tiny, digital butler. It rings when someone arrives, records packages, and can even tell you if that shadowy figure is just a friendly neighbor or… something else. But that “something else” is where things get *spooky-kawaii*—and not in a cute way.

Jamie Siminoff, Ring’s founder, keeps saying the company is serious about privacy. He emphasizes that Ring doesn’t sell customer data and that videos are stored securely. Sounds reassuring, right?  
But then comes the **facial recognition question**—and oh boy, does it get tangled. 🧶

### 🔍 The Key Points: A Knotty Situation

1. **“We don’t do facial recognition… but…”**  
   Siminoff maintains Ring *itself* doesn’t use facial recognition on customer videos. Phew! But here’s the twist: Ring allows **third-party integrations** (like certain security apps) that *can* use facial recognition if the user opts in. So while Ring’s core product might not scan faces, its ecosystem opens the door wide for others to do so. That’s like saying, “We don’t bake cookies, but we give out the recipe to anyone who asks.”

2. **Consent is a fuzzy concept**  
   The opt-in model sounds good on paper, but do users truly understand what they’re enabling? When you click “agree” on a partner app’s terms, are you aware your front-door footage might train someone else’s facial database? The burden of clarity feels heavy on us, the users holding the smartphones.

3. **The “neighbor” effect**  
   Ring’s Neighbors app encourages sharing footage with local communities and police. Even without explicit facial recognition, the ability to widely distribute video clips raises huge questions about **context, bias, and misuse**. A shared video of a “suspicious” person—with no facial tech—can still lead to real-world harm, especially for marginalized groups.
