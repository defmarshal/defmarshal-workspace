# Substack Launches a Built-In Recording Studio

*Your newsletter platform just got a major upgrade — now you can record video podcasts without ever leaving Substack.*

---

## Introduction: One Less Tool to Juggle

If you're a Substack writer, you know the drill: interview a guest, hit record on Zoom or Riverside, edit the video in Descript or CapCut, then upload the final file to Substack as a premium episode. Each extra tool means more friction, more logins, more chances for something to go wrong.

Substack's new **built‑in recording studio** changes that. Right inside your dashboard, you can now pre‑record a video conversation with up to two guests, then publish it straight to your newsletter as a paywalled episode. No more exporting, uploading, or dealing with third‑party storage limits. It's a smart move that tightens the grip on creators' workflows — and makes podcasting or video interviews accessible to anyone who can write a Substack.

---

## Key Features

### 🎥 Pre‑Record, Not Live
Unlike a live‑streaming approach, Substack's studio is designed for **pre‑recorded conversations**. Invite up to two guests, record your chat, trim the video, and hit publish. This gives you full control over quality and removes the pressure of going live — perfect for thoughtful, edited conversations.

### 👥 Multi‑Guest Support
Bring in two guests for **round‑table discussions** or panel‑style interviews. The interface handles separate video feeds, so each participant gets their own window (think Zoom grid). Great for debates, expert panels, or casual chats with friends.

### 📤 Direct Publishing to Substack
Once you're happy with the edit, the video becomes a **native Substack episode**. It's stored on Substack's infrastructure, so you don't need to worry about hosting, bandwidth, or external video platforms. Subscribers can watch right in their newsletter or app, just like any other paid post.

### 💰 Monetization Built‑In
Because the video lives inside Substack, it instantly respects your paywall settings. Free subscribers see the post blocked; paid subscribers get access automatically. No extra pay‑per‑view setup or linking out. It's another way Substack keeps the money (and the content) in‑house.

### 🛠️ Minimal Friction, Minimal Learning Curve
The studio is designed to be **approachable**. If you've used any basic video‑call app, you'll feel at home. No complex OBS setups or audio routing. Hit "record," talk, stop, trim, publish. That's it.

---

## Why This Matters

Substack isn't just adding a feature — it's **removing a barrier**. Many writers shy away from video because the tooling landscape is fragmented. By baking recording into the platform, Substack lowers the entry threshold and encourages more creators to experiment with multimedia.

It also deepens **platform lock‑in**. The more you record and publish inside Substack, the harder it becomes to move your audience elsewhere. That's a classic SaaS strategy: expand the product scope to cover the entire creative workflow.

For creators, the trade‑off is convenience vs. control. You give up advanced editing features, custom branding, and the flexibility to host elsewhere. But for many, the simplicity outweighs the limitations.

---

## Conclusion: Content Creation, Simplified

Substack's built‑in recording studio is a reminder that the best features are often the ones that **just work**. No extra accounts, no transcoding, no mysterious upload failures. Just record, trim, publish.

Will it replace professional podcast studios? Probably not for top‑tier producers. But for the thousands of writers who've been thinking, "I wish I could add video interviews without the hassle," this is a welcome gift. The platform continues to evolve from a newsletter tool into a full‑fledged media studio — one update at a time.
