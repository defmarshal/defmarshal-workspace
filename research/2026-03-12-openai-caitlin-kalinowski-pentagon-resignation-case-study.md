## Summary

In March 2026, Caitlin Kalinowski, OpenAI's Head of Robotics and Hardware, resigned in protest over the company's agreement with the U.S. Department of Defense. Her departure highlights the escalating tension between AI commercialization and ethical guardrails, particularly around military applications. Kalinowski cited insufficient policy safeguards, specifically the risks of "surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization." The controversy follows a broader industry rift: Anthropic refused similar Pentagon terms and was subsequently labeled a "supply-chain risk" by the DoD, while OpenAI moved quickly to fill the void. Consumer backlash was immediate—ChatGPT uninstalls surged 295%, and Anthropic's Claude app climbed to #1 in the U.S. App Store. This case study examines the governance failures, stakeholder reactions, and implications for responsible AI development in national security contexts.

Kalinowski's resignation points to a systemic governance breakdown:

- **Opportunistic Timing**: The sequence—Anthropic's refusal → DoD designation → OpenAI's quick deal—created perception that OpenAI prioritized market capture over principled negotiation.
- **Insufficient Red-Line Definition**: OpenAI claims "no domestic surveillance and no autonomous weapons" as red lines, but Kalinowski argues these were not sufficiently codified in the agreement. The lack of public detail fuels skepticism.

**Technical Safeguards**
OpenAI mentions "technical safeguards" but has not publicly detailed them. Possibilities include:
- Model-level restrictions (e.g., refusing prompts related to surveillance of U.S. persons or lethal autonomy).
- Audit trails and usage logging for government deployments.

However, technical enforcement is notoriously difficult; model guardrails can be jailbroken or circumvented.

**Contractual Language**
The agreement likely includes clauses prohibiting certain uses. But enforcement depends on DoD compliance and transparency—both classified, limiting external accountability.

**Red Line Ambiguity**
What constitutes "domestic surveillance"? Does it include monitoring U.S. persons abroad? What about "lethal autonomy"—does it prohibit AI recommendation systems for target selection, or only fully autonomous trigger-pull? The lack of clarity invites interpretation disputes.

### 8. Implications for AI Governance

The incident underscores several lessons:

- **Employee Voice**: Technical leaders like Kalinowski serve as ethical watchdogs. Companies should establish formal channels for raising concerns before finalizing deals.
- **Transparency by Default**: Even if contract details are classified, companies should publish their high-level policy frameworks and red lines, subject to public scrutiny.
- **Stakeholder Engagement**: Civil society, academia, and independent experts should be consulted on high-stakes AI deployments, not just government and corporate actors.
- **Market Signals**: Consumer backlash (uninstalls, App Store shifts) demonstrates that ethical reputation directly impacts user behavior. This creates a business incentive for responsible AI practices.
- **Need for Regulation**: Voluntary corporate policies are inconsistent and subject to reversal. Clear national and international regulations on military AI are needed to level the playing field and prevent a race to the bottom.

Caitlin Kalinowski's resignation is a watershed moment for AI ethics. It exposes the tension between profit and principle in one of the most strategically important sectors of the modern economy.
