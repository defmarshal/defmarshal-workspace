## Summary

Sunday, a robotics startup building a humanoid household assistant called Memo, has reached unicorn status with a $165 million Series B funding round at a $1.15 billion valuation. The round was led by Coatue Management with participation from Tiger Global, Benchmark, and Bain Capital Ventures. Founded by Stanford PhD roboticists Tony Zhao (CEO) and Cheng Chi (CTO), Sunday emerged from stealth in late 2025 and already has 1,000 people on its waitlist. The company's key differentiator is its massive real-world data collection: approximately 10 million episodes of authentic household routines captured using its patented Skill Capture Glove worn by families in over 500 homes. This data addresses the long-standing "training data problem" that has hindered general-purpose home robots. Memo is designed for domestic chores like laundry, dishwashing, table clearing, and tidying, with a rolling base for stability (not bipedal) and a silicone-clad, family-safe design. Beta deployments are planned for late 2026 with 50 Founding Family households. The valuation reflects renewed investor confidence in humanoid robots, following similar momentum with 1X's NEO robot. However, significant technical and market adoption challenges remain before household humanoids become mainstream.

**Sunday's Solution**

**Scale**

### 5. Technical Challenges Remaining

Despite the promising data collection, significant hurdles remain:

**Dexterity and Adaptability**
- Handling fragile items (wineglasses, electronics) vs. robust items (towels, dishes)
- Manipulating objects of varying sizes, weights, textures
- Un anticipated situations (spilled liquids, clutter, pets)

**Battery Life and Endurance**
- Rolling base helps with efficiency, but full-day operation requires substantial battery capacity
- Charging logistics for user households

**Cost Reduction**
- Current prototypes likely cost >$100K per unit
- Path to consumer price points (<$50K, eventually <$20K) unclear
- Scale economies may help, but hardware complexity remains high

**Reliability and Maintenance**
- Home users expect appliance-like reliability (toaster, washing machine)
- Robotics with many moving parts will require maintenance contracts
- Failure modes must be safe and easily diagnosable

**User Acceptance**
- Trust: will families leave robots unattended with children, valuables, pets?
- Privacy: robots with cameras/microphones in private spaces
- Social dynamics: robot as helper vs. intrusion
