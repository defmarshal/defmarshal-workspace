## 1. Fusion Basics Recap

Nuclear fusion powers the sun: light atomic nuclei combine to form heavier ones, releasing immense energy. On Earth, the most promising reaction is deuterium‑tritium (D‑T), yielding ~17.6 MeV per reaction. Key challenges:
- Achieve **Lawson criterion**: product of plasma density, temperature, and confinement time must exceed a threshold.
- Produce **net energy gain** (Q>1): fusion power output exceeds input power used to heat and confine plasma.
- Develop **materials** that withstand intense neutron flux (14 MeV neutrons).
- Close the **tritium breeding cycle** (tritium is radioactive and scarce; must breed in‑situ from lithium).

## 2. The ITER Project (International)

**Status 2026:**
- Assembly in Cadarache, France continues; major components delivered (toroidal field coils, vacuum vessel).
- Construction ~85% complete as of early 2026 (originally targeted 2025, delays due to component manufacturing and funding).
- **First plasma** expected 2027–2028.
- **Deuterium‑tritium operations** planned for ~2035, aiming for Q≥10 (10× input power) and 500 MW fusion output.

ITER is a proof‑of‑principle for large‑scale fusion; not designed to generate electricity for the grid. Its success will validate tokamak design for future Demo (demonstration) plants.

## 3. Compact Tokamaks & High‑Field Magnets

### Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS)

- **SPARC**: compact, high‑field tokamak using REBCO superconducting magnets (12 T field). Goal: achieve Q>2 in a device smaller than ITER.
- **Timeline:** First plasma targeted 2025–2026; net energy gain demonstration possibly 2026.
- **Follow‑on:** ARC — a conceptual 500‑MW electric pilot plant (if SPARC succeeds).
- Funding: ~$2B from investors (including Bill Gates‑backed Breakthrough Energy Ventures).
- CFS represents the most aggressive private timeline to net gain.

### Tokamak Energy (UK)

- Developing spherical tokamak with high‑temperature superconductors (HTS).
- **ST40** device aiming for Q≥1 by late 2020s.
- Collaborations with UK government and industry for future pilot plant.

## 4. Alternative Confinement Approaches

### TAE Technologies (USA)

- **Field‑Reversed Configuration (FRC):** better stability, potentially simpler engineering.
- **Norman** (100 MW, ~$500M) under construction; first plasma targeted 2026.
- **Copernicus** (500 MW) planned for late 2020s.
- TAE’s approach uses hydrogen‑boron (p‑B11) fuel (aneutronic) but that’s a longer‑term goal; initial plants likely D‑T.
- Milestone: demonstrated 100‑million‑degree C plasmas in earlier C‑2W device.

### Helion Energy (USA)

- **Pulsed FRC** approach: merging plasma rings, compressing to fusion conditions.
- **Pilot plant** (Polaris) under construction; aims to produce net electricity by 2028.
- **Of‑ftake agreement** with Microsoft for 2028 power delivery — audacious goal.
- Helion claims path to low‑cost electricity (<$30/MWh) if scaling succeeds.

### General Fusion (Canada)

- **Magnetized target fusion (MTF):** compression of plasma by pistons in liquid metal.
- Demonstration plant planned in UK; aiming for net gain mid‑2020s.
- Partnerships with Canadian and UK governments.

## 5. Inertial Confinement Fusion (ICF)

**National Ignition Facility (NIF, USA):**
- Achieved first scientific **net energy gain** (Q>1) in Dec 2022 (1.3 MJ output vs 2.05 MJ input).
- Repeated in 2023–2024 with improved yields; highest recorded ~3 MJ.
- However, laser efficiency is low (<1%), so overall wall‑plug energy gain still negative.
- Current focus: increase repetition rate, improve target manufacturing, and explore “high‑foot” pulses for robustness.
- Path to power plant: **ICF faces major engineering hurdles** (laser cost, target production rate, chamber durability). Not expected before 2040.

**Other ICF efforts:**
- **Laser Mégajoule (LMJ, France)** similar to NIF; used for stockpile stewardship and some fusion research.
- **China** (Shenguang facilities) making progress but no public net gain yet.

## 6. Long‑Duration Plasma Performance

- **EAST (China, Hefei):** Achieved 1066 s steady H‑mode operation (2021); continues to push durations.
- **KSTAR (South Korea):** Set record of 70 s H‑mode plasma at 100 million °C (2020); steady improvements.
- These are **non‑burning** plasmas; they demonstrate confinement and control but not net fusion power. Important stepping stones.

## 7. Key Challenges Remaining (2026)

1. **Materials:** First wall and blanket materials must survive high neutron flux for years without swelling, embrittlement, or activation. Reduced‑activation ferritic‑martensitic steels and SiC/SiC composites under development; testing ongoing (IFMIF‑DPF project).
2. **Tritium Breeding:** Need to breed >1 tritium per tritium burned to sustain operation. Tritium breeding experiments (e.g., Helium‑cooled lithium lead blankets) showing promise but not yet proven at scale.
3. **Economics:** Levelized cost of electricity from fusion must compete with renewables+storage ($20–$50/MWh). Capital costs of fusion plants unknown; early estimates $6,000–$10,000/kW (high). Learning curve needed.
4. **Scaling to Commercial:** From experimental devices (hundreds of MW) to gigawatt plants; from intermittent operation to high availability (>90%).
5. **Regulatory & Safety:** Fusion produces neutrons but no long‑lived high‑level waste; still requires licensing. Public perception and nuclear regulatory frameworks need adaptation.

## 9. Investment & Policy Landscape

Public‑private partnerships are accelerating (e.g., CFS with DOE, Helion with state grants). Regulatory frameworks for fusion are being drafted (e.g., US NRC rulemaking for fusion licensing).
