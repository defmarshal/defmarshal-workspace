## 1. Market Overview

LEO satellite internet delivers high-speed, low-latency broadband by deploying thousands of small satellites at 500–1,200 km altitude. Phased-array antennas track satellites; inter-satellite lasers route traffic without relying solely on ground gateways. This enables service in rural, remote, maritime, and disaster zones where terrestrial broadband is unavailable.

By early 2026:
- Starlink operates 7,000–9,300+ satellites (sources vary), total capacity ~450 Tbps, median U.S. speeds ~200 Mbps, latency 20–50ms.
- OneWeb has completed first-gen constellation of 618–648 satellites; operates at ~1,200 km altitude.
- Kuiper is ramping launches; aims for 3,236 satellites in total; initial service requires ~578 satellites for initial coverage shell.

## 2. Starlink — First Mover at Scale

### Fleet & Capacity
- Satellites in orbit: 7,000–9,300+ (multiple generations including V2)
- Throughput per V2 satellite: 80–100 Gbps
- Total network capacity: ~450 Tbps
- Customers: >6 million active (added >2.7M in past year)
- Coverage: >50 countries (some sources say 110+ countries including international plans)

### Performance & Plans
- Residential: 50–220 Mbps down, ~40 Mbps up, latency 20–50ms; Priority tier 220 Mbps guaranteed ($250/mo with 40 GB priority data then deprioritized)
- Business/Mobility: RV, maritime, aviation plans available; higher throughput options up to 500 Mbps
- Fair use: mostly unlimited; rare congestion surcharges ($500–$1,500) in saturated regions

### Pricing Trends (Jan 2026 cuts)
- Standard residential: ~$120/mo (was higher; cuts 15–40% in 12 countries)
- Starlink Lite: $49/mo up to 100 Mbps (new budget tier)
- Hardware: $299–$349 (promotions down to $175 in some markets)
- High-performance: $299/mo for up to 500 Mbps

### Competitive Advantages
- Vertical integration (SpaceX launches on Falcon 9/Starship)
- High satellite count → better signal density, fewer dropouts
- Real-world operational experience since 2020
- Strong brand and direct-to-consumer distribution

### Challenges
- Regulatory scrutiny (orbital debris, astronomy light pollution)
- Congestion in dense areas; occasional fair‑use throttling
- Bulkier user terminals (though Gen3 improved)

## 5. Competitive Dynamics & Pricing Pressure

- Starlink’s Jan 2026 price cuts (15–40% in 12 countries) are a pre-emptive move ahead of Kuiper’s launch and OneWeb’s enterprise expansion.
- Analysts interpret this as “land grab” to establish subscriber bases and switching costs.
- Kuiper’s sub‑$400 hardware and potential AWS bundling could pressure Starlink further, especially in price‑sensitive markets.
- OneWeb avoids direct consumer competition, focusing on enterprise/wholesale where Starlink’s mass‑market model is less relevant.
- Traditional GEO players (Viasat, HughesNet) losing ground to LEO on speed/latency; Viasat Unleashed plans remain a budget option but with ~600ms latency.

## 6. Technology & Operational Trends

- **Satellite capacity:** Gen2/Gen3 satellites now 80–100 Gbps each; inter‑satellite laser links standard.
- **User terminals:** Phased‑array antennas becoming cheaper (Starlink’s Gen3 manufacturing cut costs ~50% since 2023).
- **Network orchestration:** Software-defined dynamic beam allocation to manage congestion and prioritize critical users.
- **Orbital debris concerns:** growing scrutiny; FCC and international bodies pressuring operators for sustainable disposal (Starlink investing in de‑orbit capabilities).
